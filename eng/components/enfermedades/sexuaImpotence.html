    Sexual impotence

    Impotence is the constant inability to maintain sufficient erection for intercourse. Doctors often use the term "erectile dysfunction" to describe this disorder and differentiate it from other problems that hinder intercourse, such as lack of sexual desire and problems with ejaculation and orgasm.

    Impotence may be the total disability to achieve erection, irregular disability to achieve it or a tendency to maintain only short erections. Because of these variations, it is difficult to define impotence and calculate its incidence.

    Between 10 and 15 million American men suffer from helplessness; The incidence increases with age: approximately 5% of men at 40 and between 15 and 25% of men at 65 experience impotence. Even so, it is not an inevitable part of aging.

    As erection requires a sequence of phenomena, impotence can occur when any of these is interrupted.

    The sequence includes the nerve impulses of the brain, the spinal cord and the penis area, and the response of the muscles, the fibrous tissue, the veins and arteries of the cavernous bodies and nearby areas.

    The most common cause of impotence is the damage of the arteries, smooth muscle and fibrous tissue, often as a result of a disease:

    • Diabetes mellitus
    • Kidney disease
    • Chronic alcoholism
    • Multiple sclerosis
    • Atherosclerosis
    • Abdominal aneurysm
    • Surgical interventions (prostate)
    • Penis lesions, spinal cord, prostate, urinary bladder and pelvis
    • Many common medications
    • Psychological factors (stress, anxiety, sense of guilt, depression, little self -esteem)
    • Hormonal abnormalities (insufficient testosterone production)
    • smoking
